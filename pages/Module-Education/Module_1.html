Educational Module 1: What is an HSA?

LEARNING OBJECTIVES

  • Understand what a Health Savings Account (HSA) is
  • Explain the purpose and benefits of an HSA
  • Know how HSAs can support financial planning

LESSON

A Health Savings Account (HSA) is a personal savings account that helps you pay for medical expenses. Think of it as a health-focused savings fund that you control.

HSAs are individual accounts, meaning they belong only to you and cannot be shared or joint.

HSAs have a Triple-Tax Advantage, where you can deposit money into your HSA and let it grow tax-free. The money is always yours, and it rolls over every year, so there is no pressure to spend it all before a deadline.

Tax-Free Contributions: Every dollar you put into your HSA lowers your taxable income. If you put in $1,000, the IRS acts like you never earned that money, so you don't pay taxes on it.

Tax-Free Growth: If your HSA sits in the bank and earns interest (or if you choose to invest it in the stock market), you keep 100% of the gains. You don’t have to report or pay taxes on that growth.

Tax-Free Out: When you spend the money on things like doctor visits, prescriptions, or even a new pair of glasses, you don’t pay any taxes on the withdrawal. It’s like a permanent 20–30% discount on your healthcare.

You can use HSA funds to pay for many everyday healthcare costs, including:

  • Doctor visits
  • Prescription medication
  • Dental and vision care
  • Mental health services
  • Lab tests, medical procedures, and other qualified expenses

One of the biggest benefits of an HSA is flexibility. There is no age requirement to open or use one, and you can keep your account even if you change jobs or move states.

So, Why Is an HSA Important?

Health expenses can be unpredictable, especially as you transition out of a student health plan or off your parents' insurance. An HSA helps you prepare for unexpected costs while also giving you a smart way to save for future healthcare needs.

Starting early allows your savings to grow over time, making HSAs a powerful tool for both short-term budgeting and long-term financial planning.
